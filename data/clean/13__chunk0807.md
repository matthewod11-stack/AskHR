# ABSTRACT

The study of humor has a long tradition in philosophy, sociology, psychology, anthropology, and communications. Evidence from these ﬁelds suggests that humor can have effects on creativity, cohesiveness, and performance, but organizational scholars have paid it relatively little attention. We hope to ‘‘jump-start’’ such a research program. To do this, we ﬁrst outline the theoretical rationale underlying the production and appreciation of humor, namely, its motivational, cognitive, and emotional mechanisms. Next, we review the literature linking humor to creativity, cohesiveness, and other performance-relevant outcomes. In particular, we note how this literature is theoretically well-grounded, but that the empirical ﬁndings are largely correlational and/or based on qualitative research designs.
